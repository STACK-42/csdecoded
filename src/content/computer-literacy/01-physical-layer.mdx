# The Physical Layer

Everything about computing is, at its core, a story of interaction. A human does something, the machine responds, and that loop continues. The machine itself has no intention, no awareness — it only reacts to structured input. The quality of your interaction defines the quality of your outcome.

Most people never fully internalize this. They treat computers as tools that sometimes work and sometimes do not, without realizing that **fluency matters**. Each layer of fluency removes friction between your intention and execution. The first layer is physical — where your hands meet the machine.

## Typing

Typing sits at the center of this layer, and it is far more important than it seems. When you type slowly, your thoughts are constantly interrupted by the mechanics of expression, and that creates frustration. Once you reach something like sixty words per minute, you begin to think and write almost at the same time, and at higher speeds like ninety, the keyboard disappears as a barrier.

The real shift is not raw speed. It is **removing the cognitive load of searching for keys**, so your brain stays with the idea instead of the tool.

<Callout tone="orange" label="The test">
You are fluent enough when you can finish typing a sentence whose end you had not yet planned when you started.
</Callout>

## Shortcuts

I remember when my mouse used to lag, and I was forced to rely on the keyboard. That constraint revealed something powerful: almost everything on a computer can be done without leaving the keyboard, and once you internalize that, your flow changes completely.

You stop breaking your thinking to navigate. Instead, you move continuously through tasks. People who live in shortcuts are not just faster, they are **more coherent** in how they think while working — because their tools stop interrupting the thought.
