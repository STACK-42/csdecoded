# The One-Week Plan

There is no perfect plan. I want to say that first because the internet is full of color coded weekly schedules that look very organized and have nothing to do with how exams actually feel when you are inside them.

it varies from one to another the way we operate, the time we have and almost everything. So pinning a schedule is really not that wise. What there is, instead, are principles. Things that hold regardless of your subject, your schedule, or how badly you have been preparing. The plan you build has to be yours, but it has to be built on something real.

## Before You Plan Anything: Build the Map

The first thing I do before I touch any material is get a picture of the whole subject in my head. Not the details. Not the equations. Just the shape of it, what it is fundamentally about, how many topics it has, and roughly how much weight each one carries.

This sounds like (why would I do that thing) and as if it is somethign small. It is not.

When you sit down to study something without this picture, your brain is doing two jobs at once: learning the material and trying to figure out what the material even is. That second job runs in the background the whole time, adding a layer of stress that has nothing to do with the actual content. You feel overwhelmed not because the subject is impossible, but because you cannot see its edges, becuase you really never knew what it talks about.

A practical way to do this is to take the course name, outline, or the table of contents, or whatever map the subject gives you, and read it in one sitting without studying anything. Just understand the structure. How many main topics? What connects them? What is the central idea the whole subject is built around? LLMs can help with that today.

<Callout tone="violet" label="The Feynman test">
Take each main topic and the course idea and try to explain them out loud, in your own words, as simply as possible, as if you are talking to someone who has never heard of it. Not to impress them, but to test yourself. If you cannot do this without vague language, you do not understand it yet. That is where you start.
</Callout>

This matters even more for technical subjects. A student studying numerical analysis who memorizes methods without understanding that the whole subject is about *approximation*, finding values close enough to be useful when exact values are impossible, will struggle with every new problem type. But the student who understands that core idea has a mental framework that catches almost everything else underneath it.

The map does not take long. less than an hour, an hour, maybe two if you are really slow. but that's a real investment, I can't skip this part at all, It will save you far more than that later.

## Now Plan the Week

Once you have the map, you can actually plan. Here is how I think about it.

**Start with a checklist.** Take every topic you need to cover and write it down. All of it. One of the main sources of stress during finals is not the studying itself, it is the background feeling of not knowing how much is left. A visible checklist removes that. You cross things off. The weight gets smaller in a way you can actually see. and most important, you can estimate and plan for the time based on that.

**Read the subject order and plan backwards from the exam date.** This sounds obvious but again to confirm. The first exam on your schedule gets the most preparation time. Do not start with the last one because you like it more, it feels easier or its the hardest. You study in the order the calendar gives you, and you fit the rest around it.

**If one subject is very large and the exam is not first**, slice it. Block some hours for it across the days you are preparing for other subjects. A subject that gets two focused hours every other day for a week is in much better shape than one that gets eight hours the night before. We covered [why](./01-the-problem-with-how-you-study.mdx#your-brain-consolidates-during-rest) in the first lesson, your brain consolidates during rest, and you need that to happen more than once.

**Prioritize by two things**: topics that appear most often in past exams, and topics where you are weakest. are not always the same list, and both matter. A topic you understand well but rarely appears is not where your time and effort goes. A topic you do not understand that shows up every year, that is where you spend your time.

<Callout tone="orange" label="A practical shortcut">
Take the past two to five years of exams for the subject and give them to any LLM. Ask it to identify the topics that appear most consistently across years. this is reading the pattern. Exams are written by humans with habits, and those habits are visible in the archive.
</Callout>

## The Environment Is Not a Detail

Some people have environment techniques, Pomodoro is a good one, I do not use the Pomodoro technique personally. I study in one hour blocks and that works for me. But what I am very firm about is removing the phone from the room entirely. Not turning it face down if you find yourself holding it each 5 minutes.

The [research](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/full/10.1086/691462) on this is clear: the mere presence of a smartphone on the desk, even turned off, reduces available cognitive capacity. Your brain allocates a small but real amount of attention to resisting the impulse to check it. That attention is not available for studying.

This is called **attentional residue**, and learning this concept a year ago, clicked for me, when part of your mind stays attached to something else while you are trying to focus on the task in front of you. The phone does not have to be on for this to happen. It just has to be there.

Remove it. Use website blockers if you need them. Protect the hours you actually sit down to work, because they are fewer than they feel.

## What to Do the Night Before

Very little that is new.

The night before an exam or the very few hours are not for learning. I hate to break it for you, but your professor was right, It is for light review, going over your notes, testing yourself on things you already know, confirming your understanding. If you encounter something completely unfamiliar the night before, do not try to learn it from scratch. Flag it, note that it exists, and move on. New material introduced under stress the night before an exam rarely survives to the exam itself.

What you actually need the night before is sleep. that it, is the biology. Memory consolidation happens during sleep, and specifically during the deep stages of it. Staying up until three in the morning to read the same chapter a fourth time is trading the consolidation of everything you already know for a few hours of anxious scanning. and I think you know what it is like to go sober for the exam.

<Callout tone="mint" label="The honest summary">
Build the map. Make the checklist. Study in the order the calendar demands, not the order of your comfort. Protect your focus hours. Sleep before the exam. These are not revolutionary ideas, but most people ignore at least two of them, and that is where the marks go.
</Callout>
