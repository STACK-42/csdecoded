# Stress & Anxiety

You studied. You slept. You knew the material the night before. And then you sat down in that room, read the first question, and something happened. It felt like the material went somewhere else, or started to fade. You knew that you knew it, but it was not there. You somehow lost the grip on what you thought you understood. You started overthinking it, fearing it.

Understanding this changes how you experience exams, and this directly affects how you prepare for them.

## Introducing Cortisol

When your brain detects a threat—any threat, whether it is a dog running towards you or an exam paper—it triggers the release of cortisol. This is a steroid hormone produced by your adrenal glands, and its job is to prepare your body to deal with the threat in front of it. This is when you feel alert, heart rate goes up, and blood flow redirects toward your muscles. You might feel restless or tense as your attention narrows.

A good way to spot cortisol is by logging emotions. I have been using the [How We Feel App](https://howwefeel.org/) for around 4 years now. It took me years to map that "restless" and "tense" are just sensations related to cortisol. Having an Apple Watch helped too—there are smart apps that track your stress levels. When both combined, I became much better at spotting when I'm stressed and managing my cortisol.

![How We Feel Screenshot](https://res.cloudinary.com/dkmutnahg/image/upload/v1778884276/IMG_2646_nqstso.jpg)
*Screenshot from my Account*

That system isn't "broken"—it's biology. It kept your ancestors alive. The problem is that it was built for a different kind of threat. When the threat is a lion, narrowed attention and physical readiness are exactly what you need. When the threat is a question about numerical methods, they are the last thing you need.

The prefrontal cortex—the part of your brain responsible for complex reasoning, working memory, and the ability to retrieve information—is suppressed under high cortisol. Read that again: it is not damaged or erased; it is **suppressed**. The brain is not being stupid; it is running the right program for the wrong situation.

<Callout tone="orange" label="What blanking actually is">
When your mind suddenly feels empty in an exam—like you are staring at a question you studied but nothing comes out—it is cortisol. It has narrowed your attention and partially suppressed the prefrontal cortex. The information is still there, but access is temporarily reduced. This is a physiological event, not a signal that you don't know the material.
</Callout>

## Balancing Extremes of Anxiety

Cortisol does not simply make things worse as it rises. The relationship between stress and performance follows what psychologists call the **Yerkes-Dodson curve**—an inverted U shape.

![Yerkes-Dodson curve](https://res.cloudinary.com/dkmutnahg/image/upload/v1778893904/graph.002_ayktx3.png)
[Image Source: Diamond D.M., et al. (2007)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1906714/)

Too little stress and you perform poorly because you are not alert or motivated. Too much stress and you perform poorly because your system is overwhelmed. The peak of performance sits in the middle: enough pressure to focus, but not enough to flood the system.

This means a small amount of exam anxiety is not your enemy—it's what makes you sharp. The problem is when anxiety crosses the threshold into the high-cortisol zone, and your body starts treating the exam like a survival situation.

Most struggle under pressure is not a sign of weakness. Students struggle because they don't realize it's about balancing extremes. They interpret every spike of anxiety as evidence that something is wrong, which produces more anxiety, pushing cortisol even higher. The spiral is self-reinforcing.

## Negativity Bias

There is a second problem that happens during exams that compounds the cortisol and anxiety problem: **negativity bias**.

The brain does not treat all information equally. It assigns more weight, more attention, and more memory to negative experiences than to positive ones. In an exam, your brain is actively working against your sense of your own performance.

You might finish an exam having answered nineteen questions well and one question badly. What you replay on the walk home is the one you got wrong. You reconstruct it, overthink it, and catastrophize about what it means for your grade. The nineteen correct answers exist in your memory but carry no weight. The one wrong answer was enough to trigger an emotional response with texture and weight.

This is a structural feature of how the brain processes experience. Knowing it exists gives you the ability to work around it.

<Callout tone="violet" label="The negativity bias in real time">
When you leave an exam convinced you failed, ask yourself: *Am I remembering everything, or am I remembering only what went wrong?* These are not the same thing. Your brain will always make the negative more available than the positive. Account for that before you reach a conclusion.
</Callout>

## "I Have Never Seen This Before"

This is the moment where most damage happens. You hit a question that looks completely unfamiliar. Your first reaction is usually panic.

What happens next is often the wrong thing: freezing, spending too long on that one question, or deciding the exam is lost and giving up on the rest.

Here is the truth: exams from serious institutions almost always contain questions that most students won't fully solve. They are there to separate grades, not to trick you. A question you cannot answer is a normal part of exam design. The students who perform best move past it without letting it contaminate the rest of their performance.

The practical response is mechanical: **mark it, leave it, continue.** Come back at the end if time allows. This is difficult to do under cortisol because your brain wants to resolve the threat before moving on. Practicing this response—even just knowing it's your plan—makes it more available when you need it.

<Callout tone="mint" label="The reframe that actually helps">
An unfamiliar question is not a sign that you are unprepared. It is a sign that the exam is doing its job. Your job is to perform well on everything else and not let one difficult question borrow time and attention from the questions you can answer.
</Callout>

## What You Can Actually Do

**Before the exam:** The most effective intervention for cortisol is the quality of your preparation. Retrieval practice builds genuine confidence grounded in evidence. Quality of sleep is an important factor too.

**In the exam room:** If you feel an anxiety spike, the single most effective physiological intervention is slow, extended exhaling (e.g., 4-second inhale, 8-second exhale). This activates the parasympathetic nervous system. This technique is called **physiological sighing**, studied at Stanford by Andrew Huberman's lab.

**After the exam:** Give your negativity bias a number. Ask yourself: *How many questions did I handle reasonably well?* Force an estimate. The number will almost always be higher than what your emotional state suggests.
