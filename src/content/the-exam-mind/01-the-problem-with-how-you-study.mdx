# The Problem With How You Study

## On the Memory of This Course

I have a sister named Luzan. She is my eldest sister, and when I was a child I was the opposite of her in almost every way, playful, barely studying, averge throughout my whole journey in school, never really committing to what the schooling system wanted from me. I was good at making teachers and classmates happy. I could perform well socially, I could get assignments done. But exams were a different thing. Exams had a weight I did not know what to do with.

My mother used to mention Luzan whenever I stumbled. as a reference point I could not ignore. She would tell me that even as a little girl, during exam season, when all the other students were panicking and cramming and barely sleeping, Luzan would be watching television. Calm. Not indifferent, she was a good student, but genuinely unbothered. That continued through university. Girls around her would be losing sleep and she would be having a normal evening.

I heard this story so many times it started to sound like a myth. Something you tell children to make them feel worse about themselves, or a rare quality that some people are simply born with and others are not. By the time I was old enough to actually observe Luzan's life up close, she had already finished university. I never got to watch it in real time.

But I kept thinking about it. And as I got older and started paying attention to how people actually perform under pressure — not just in exams, but in anything that matters — I started to understand that what Luzan had was not a gift. It was a relationship with pressure that most people never build, because nobody shows them how. Fear was not what moved her. And that is learnable.

That realization is part of what made me want to write this course.

## Studying That Feels Productive

You probably already know how to study. Read the material. Make notes. Highlight. Maybe re-read. Maybe make a summary. Repeat until the exam.

This is what I love the call "feels productive". It is not.

it has nothing to do with you, no you being lazy or doing it wrong in some obvious way, but because the way most students study is built around a feeling more than a result. The feeling is *familiarity*. The result you actually need is *recall*. These two things are not the same, and confusing them is where most exam performance is lost.

## The Asset (Memory)

A German psychologist named [Hermann Ebbinghaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Ebbinghaus) spent years memorizing nonsense syllables, sequences like "DAX" and "BOK", and then testing himself at different intervals to see how fast he forgot them. Nobody had done this before. The result is now called the **forgetting curve**, and it is one of the most replicated findings in all of psychology.

![Description of image](https://res.cloudinary.com/dkmutnahg/image/upload/v1778579659/forgetting-curve-mosalingua_q1fipb.jpg)

Without any review, you forget roughly half of what you learned within the first hour. By the next day, you remember maybe 30%. Within a week, almost nothing.

it suprises me that this was in 1885. it is there and we have known this for over a century. And yet the people are doing the same standard exam preparation, read everything the night before, as if this curve does not exist.

<Callout tone="orange" label="The core problem">
Re-reading feels like studying because the material looks familiar. But familiarity is not the same as being able to retrieve something under pressure. Your brain is recognizing, not recalling. The exam asks you to recall.
</Callout>

## How Memory Actually Works

What I know is that your memory is not a safe storage room where you just put things and later retrieve them **unchanged**. It is closer to a muscle — it gets stronger the more you *use* it, and weaker when you only *look* at it.

Every time you successfully recall something — pull it out of your memory without seeing it in front of you — you strengthen the connection. Every time you simply re-read it, you strengthen nothing, because the information is right there and your brain does not need to do the work.

This is what cognitive scientists call **retrieval practice**, and the research on it is overwhelming. Testing yourself is not just a way to check what you know. It is itself the act of learning. The effort of reaching for an answer, even when you get it wrong, is what makes it stick.

There is also a second mechanism: **spacing**. Reviewing material across multiple sessions — with time between them — is dramatically more effective than reviewing the same material for the same total time in one sitting. Your brain consolidates memory during rest. Cramming the night before skips that consolidation almost entirely.

<Callout tone="violet" label="The two mechanisms that actually work">
**Retrieval practice** — test yourself instead of re-reading. **Spaced repetition** — spread your sessions over days, not hours. These two things together are the most researched study interventions in education psychology.
</Callout>

## Why You Keep Studying the Wrong Way

This is not a character flaw. There is a reason.

When you re-read a chapter and it feels familiar, your brain gives you a signal that says *I know this*. That signal feels good. It feels like progress. Psychologists call this **the fluency illusion** — the easier information is to process, the more confident you feel about knowing it, even when that confidence is not connected to real retention.

Testing yourself is uncomfortable by comparison. You reach for something and it is not there. You feel like you do not know the material. This feeling is actually the learning happening — but it feels like failure, so students avoid it.

Hard studying that produces discomfort is often more effective than comfortable studying that produces nothing.
